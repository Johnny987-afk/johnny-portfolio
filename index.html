01 · About me & this case

Designer & gamer

I’m Johnny Ju, a UX designer and researcher – and also a pretty typical gamer. I’m interested in the messy, emotional side of gaming behavior: why we scroll libraries, postpone “big” games, and sometimes choose YouTube over the titles we were once excited about.

In my work I like to combine human-centered research with visual storytelling, turning personal habits into clear patterns and design opportunities.

This case started from my own growing backlog and spread to friends and online communities – which is why I decided to treat it as a UX problem, not just a personal flaw.

02 · Problem & questions

From “pile of shame” to design question

Many young gamers have a digital “pile of shame” – games they were excited enough to buy, but never actually play. Libraries keep growing through sales, bundles, and hype, while real time and energy stay limited.

Emotionally, this creates a mix of guilt, FOMO, and sometimes quiet pride in having a “serious” library. I wanted to understand what sits underneath this behavior.

That turned into a set of guiding questions:

  • What drives young people to buy games they don’t end up playing?
  • What gets in the way of actually starting those games?
  • How do they feel about their backlogs emotionally?
  • Where can design help shift from “buy → ignore” to “buy → play”?
Quick view

Problem

Unplayed games, growing backlogs, and guilt around “wasted” purchases.

Design challenge

Support players in actually using what they already own, without shaming them for how they got there.

03 · Approach

Survey · interviews · forums

I mixed lightweight quantitative and qualitative methods to understand both scale and stories.

  • Survey (~50 gamers, 16–25) – library size, unplayed rate, top reasons for not playing.
  • 6 in-depth interviews – purchase stories, how people plan to play, what actually happens, and how they feel about backlog games.
  • Online observation – forum threads and posts about Steam sales, “game hoarding,” and backlog guilt.

Together, these showed that my personal pattern wasn’t unique – it was a fairly normal part of modern digital game ownership.

04 · Key insights

Why we buy, why we don’t play

Insight 1 – FOMO & sales

Deep discounts, bundles, and “limited time” offers create strong FOMO. Many purchases are driven by the deal and the fantasy of “future me will definitely want this,” not by a real plan to play soon.

Insight 2 – Time & heaviness

Big games demand serious time and mental energy. Students and young adults overestimate their free time and keep waiting for a “perfect moment” that rarely arrives, so games remain unlaunched even when they still care about them.

Insight 3 – Social hype

Friends and streamers strongly influence purchases. Buying a game is a way to feel included in a conversation. But if the timing or fit isn’t right, the social energy fades before people ever start playing.

Insight 4 – Collecting & identity

For some players, owning games is its own hobby. A big library feels like part of their identity as “a real gamer.” Buying gives a quick dopamine hit and a sense of future possibility – even if they know they may not play everything.

The behavior looks irrational from the outside, but makes emotional sense when you consider identity, social belonging, and limited time.

05 · Design response

Backlog Buddy: suggest · schedule · social

Instead of trying to stop people from buying games, I focused on closing the gap between owning and playing.

I explored several directions and converged on a concept called Backlog Buddy – a companion feature that connects to your existing libraries and helps you decide what to play next from what you already own.

  • Suggest – a “Play Next” suggestion that surfaces one unplayed game so you don’t drown in choices.
  • Schedule – the option to block a small session for that game and get a reminder when it’s time.
  • Social – see which friends also own it and invite them to tackle a backlog game together.
  • Challenge & progress – light goals like “clear 2 backlog games this month,” with progress and small celebrations.

The UI follows a dark, game-like style so it feels at home next to platforms like Steam or console dashboards, with more emphasis on progress than guilt.

06 · Reflection & what’s next

Emotional logic & gentle UX

This project reminded me that behavior which looks irrational from the outside often has very clear emotional logic. Buying games and not playing them is tied to identity, social belonging, FOMO, and the comfort of “maybe later,” not just laziness.

It also reinforced my belief that good UX should support people gently rather than scold them. Backlog Buddy is designed around small wins, clearer choices, and support from friends – not shame.

If I continue this project, the next step would be to test the concept with real players and see if it changes how they interact with their backlog. I’m also curious about applying similar ideas to other “buy but don’t use” spaces like online courses or books.

Where I’m headed

I want to keep exploring motivation, emotion, and behavior change in everyday digital experiences – especially around entertainment, learning, and wellbeing.
